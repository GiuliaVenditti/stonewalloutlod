About

The Stonewall Inn Project is a web resource our team has developed for the Knowledge Organization and Cultural Heritage course at Alma Mater Studiorum Bologna, held by Professor Francesca Tomasi. Our aim is to semantically represent on the web an historical event as a collective manifestation by describing meaningful relationships between cultural objects, people and places that either influenced or were influenced by the riots themselves.

Gay Liberation Front

The idea.

As our study domain we choose the Stonewall Inn’s riots since they are widely represented by and representative of today's cultural environment . In the night between the 27th and 28th of June 1969 the gay community of the West Village (NY) finally reacted against police brutality, standing, for the first time after 2,500 years, for their rights to exist, to express themselves and to be recognized as people and not just as freaks or over-sexualized objects. Riots lasted for more days at Greenwich Village, but evolved into a pacific demonstration, involving a big slice of the New Yorkers. All of this happened at the iconic gay bar Stonewall Inn, that is recognised today as a significative place related to the famous Gay Pride parade in New York City . Our project aims at faithfully representing the riots, both under a cultural point of view, by means of their effects on society and pop-culture, and an historical point of view, representing some of the cultural objects that both drove and have been driven by Stonewall’s events.

This was the Rosa Parks moment, the time that gay people stood up and said no. And once that happened, the whole house of cards that was the system of oppression of gay people started to crumble.

Lucian Truscott

Items description.

The chosen items have been selected in the attempt to represent the Stonewall riots in an historical dimension, including both some artifacts describing the context in which the riots took place and some that represent the effects it had on culture and society. The inclusion of multiple different cultural objects in our domain was also meant to highlight the capillarity and transmediality of ideas and feelings that directly inspired or arose from the riots.

1. New York City Gay Scene Guide Vol. 2

Book

1969 guide to New York gay scene which included information on bars, baths, coffee houses, clothing shops, hotels, discussion groups, theatres, night clubs, parks, private clubs, restaurants and homophile organizations

2. Stonewall Outloud

Short Movie

Contemporary voices in the LGBTQ community discuss 'Stonewall Outloud', the first documentary to chronicle the historic 1969 event that sparked the gay liberation movement

3. Over The Rainbow

Song

"Over the Rainbow" is a ballad composed by Harold Arlen with lyrics by Yip Harburg. It was written for the 1939 film The Wizard of Oz and was sung by actress Judy Garland in her starring role as Dorothy Gale. It won the Academy Award for Best Original Song and became Garland's signature song

4. Gay Power Graffiti

Murales

Handwritten chalk text on a boarded-up window of the Stonewall Inn (53 Christopher Street) after riots over the weekend of June 27, 1969. The text reads 'Gay Prohibition Corupts (sic) Cops and Feeds Mafia'

5. Y'all Better Quiet Down

Audio Recording

Recording of the speech given by Sylvia Rivera at the 4th Annual Christopher Street Liberation Day Rally in 1973

6. Rainbow Flag

Design Product

The Rainbow Flag (also known as the gay pride flag or LGBT pride flag) is a symbol of lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and queer (LGBTQ) pride and LGBTQ social movements

7. Full Moon Over The Stonewall Inn

Article

Voice columnist Howard Smith’s account of the night he spent on “the wrong side of the blue line,” barricaded inside the Stonewall Inn with police

8. RuPaul's Drag Race

TV Show

 American reality competition television series, the first in the Drag Race franchise, produced by World of Wonder for Logo TV, WOW Presents Plus, and, beginning with the ninth season, VH1

9. Intro 475 demonstration at City Hall, NYC

Photography

 Photograph of Sylvia Rivera, Marsha P. Johnson,and a group of other activists, shot by Diana Davies

10. Stonewall National Monument

Landmark

 Stonewall National Monument is a 7.7-acre (3.1 ha) U.S. National Monument in the West Village neighborhood of Greenwich Village in Lower Manhattan, New York City

Theoretial Model.

During the development of our theoretical model we based its structure on four sections determined by the following questions: Who?, When?, Where? and What?, as suggested by the project’s guidelines. We also revised, enriched and generalized our first set of questions and properties contained in the first three sections to make it clearer and keep it coherent with our initial idea. The What? section is the one containing those properties that are more characterizing of the objects and that we couldn’t generalize in order to provide a punctual description of the single item.

Who?

The main roles related to people are connected to their agency, intended as their participation in the life-cycle of the event and its related objects. The roles highlighted by this approach are connected to designation, creation, contribution and dissemination of cultural objects, and are better identified within a general frame of sequential or parallel influences to the conceptual streams that convey in the designed model. For this reason we decided to employ different standards such as DC/DCTERMS for general properties, e.g. dc:creator and dc:publisher for marking those agents having an active role in the creation of the item and its release to the public. MO, MARC21, ECRM and the BBC’s Programme Ontology have been used to distinguish between more specific properties related to the type of object we want to describe. Finally properties from CiTO have been exploited for expressing citing and cited entities, while DbPedia is used for marking symbolic relations.

When?

The project domain, considering its causes and effects, covers a vast temporal space. The large timespan we are considering comprises both objects and people that can be chronologically located before and after the central historical event as shown in the mind map. Furthermore the heterogeneous nature of the objects needed a conceptualization of time defined on one hand as a punctual entity, e.g. the date a photo was taken, and on the other hand as a timespan, e.g. the broadcasting period of a Tv show. Schema.org was for our purpose the best ontology because it is applicable to a wide range of elements and covers all concepts related to time that we needed. The property 362 belonging to MARC-21 is used to give additional information about designation of landmarks.

Where?

The historical event that is at the core of our project happens to be named after its specific location, but spatial dimensions of its context and impact are much wider and differentiated. For this reason, places have been distinguished as objects’ locations, settings of events directly related to an item and finally as dissemination environments such as web sites. The ontologies implied in this description include Schema for covering general aspects related to spatial location, alongside with more specialized ontologies such as MODS and MARC-21.

What?

In a Linked Open Data environment, rather than a single event clearly definable by its span in space and time, The Stonewall Riots are preferable to be considered as a convergence of factors generated from the actions of some individuals gathered together as a community. The manifestation of the causes and effects as a series of entities apparently unrelated to each other: from this point of view the main goal of our resource can be identified as the unravelling of the undergoing ‘red thread’ that manifests in the relationships between cultural objects collected and interpreted as a mirror of the feelings and thoughts that generated and manifested in the events of Stonewall. To do so we decided to pose for each type of item some more specific questions: PO by BBC is used to describe genres of video material and the broadcaster such as VH1 and Youtube Originals; MARC-21 for bibliographic records describes the properties of textual materials; the features of the visual artworks included in pour project are mainly described using ECMR vocabulary by CIDOC, while the GVP ontology by Getty was employed for expressing its context artwork. For describing audio materials we initially thought about employing the Music Ontology, but since we wanted to include into the same object type different kinds of items such as musical songs and archival material, we opted for more general ontologies such as Schema.
